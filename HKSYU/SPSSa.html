Master the fundamentals of data exploration and scale analysis

Descriptive Statistics

Learn to calculate and interpret basic descriptive statistics including frequency, central tendency, and measures of dispersion.

Primary Dataset: gss2002-sample2.sav

SPSS Steps:

  1. Open dataset: gss2002-sample2.sav
  2. Click Analyze → Descriptive Statistics → Frequencies
  3. Select variables: Age of respondents, How often attends religious services, RS religious preference
  4. Click Statistics button
  5. Check: Mean, Median, Mode
  6. Click Continue, then OK

SPSS Steps:

  1. Open dataset: gss2002-sample2.sav
  2. Click Analyze → Descriptive Statistics → Descriptives
  3. Select variables: age, highest year of school completed, hours per day watching tv
  4. Click Options button
  5. Check: Mean, Std. deviation, Minimum, Maximum
  6. Click Continue, then OK

Key Insight: A larger standard deviation (SD) implies behaviors are more diverse in the sample.

SPSS Steps:

  1. Open dataset: gss2002-sample2.sav
  2. Click Analyze → Descriptive Statistics → Frequencies
  3. Select variable: age

  5. Check: Quartiles, Percentiles

Charts and Visualizations

Create and interpret various charts to visually display and analyze your data.

Primary Dataset: gssnet.sav

SPSS Steps:

  1. Open dataset: gssnet.sav
  2. Click Graphs → Legacy Dialogs → Histogram
  3. Select variable: Age
  4. Check "Display normal curve"
  5. Click OK
  6. Repeat the process for Education (Highest year of school completed)
Interpreting Distribution Shape
  • Skewed right (positive skew): When mean > median, data has a tail extending to the right
  • Skewed left (negative skew): When mean < median, data has a tail extending to the left
  • Outlier caution: When extreme values exist, mean may not be the best measure of central tendency

SPSS Steps for Bar Chart:

  1. Open dataset: gssnet.sav
  2. Click Graphs → Legacy Dialogs → Bar
  3. Select Simple and Summaries for groups of cases

  2. Select Summaries for groups of cases

Scaling: Reliability & Factor Analysis

Learn to assess scale reliability and perform factor analysis to determine underlying dimensions in your data.

Primary Dataset: spss 1314 data.sav

SPSS Steps for Reliability Analysis:

  1. Open dataset: spss 1314 data.sav
  2. Click Analyze → Scale → Reliability Analysis
  3. Select all self-esteem variables (self-esteem 1 to self-esteem 10)
  4. Ensure Model is set to "Alpha"
  5. Click Statistics
  6. Check "Item", "Scale", and "Scale if item deleted"
  7. Click Continue, then OK
Handling Reverse-Coded Items
  1. Click Transform → Recode into Different Variables
  2. Select the reverse-coded items
  3. Click Old and New Values
  4. Enter each value pair (e.g., 0→3, 1→2, 2→1, 3→0)
  5. Click Add for each pair
  6. Provide output variable names
  7. Click Continue, then OK
  8. Rerun Reliability Analysis with recoded items

Interpreting Cronbach's Alpha:

  • 0.7 is acceptable reliability
  • 0.8 is good reliability
  • 0.9 is very good reliability
  • 0.6 may be acceptable in exploratory research

SPSS Steps for Factor Analysis:

  1. Open dataset: spss 1314 data.sav
  2. Click Analyze → Dimension Reduction → Factor
  3. Select social network variables (social network1 to social network 10)
  4. Click Descriptives
  5. Check: Univariate, Initial solution, Coefficients
  6. Click Continue
  7. Click Extraction
  8. Ensure Method is "Principal components"
  9. Check: Unrotated factor solution, Scree plot
  10. Select "Correlation matrix"
  11. Select "Eigenvalues over 1"
  12. Click Continue
  13. Click Rotation
  14. Select "Varimax"
  15. Check "Rotated solution"
  16. Click Continue, then OK
Key Factor Analysis Requirements
  • Variables: Must be inter-related with correlations r > 0.3
  • Sample size: Should be more than 100
  • Factor loadings: Look for values > 0.5
  • Eigenvalues: Consider factors with eigenvalues > 1
  • Variance explained: Solution should explain > 60% of cumulative variance
  • Scree plot: Look for the point where the curve flattens

SPSS2: Statistical Tests & Correlations

Master advanced statistical techniques to analyze relationships between variables

Independent Samples t-test

Test for significant differences between two independent groups on a continuous outcome variable.

Primary Dataset: gssnet.sav

SPSS Steps:

  1. Open dataset: gssnet.sav
  2. Click Analyze → Compare Means → Independent-Samples T Test
  3. Select dependent variable (continuous outcome measure)
  4. Select grouping variable (categorical with 2 groups)
  5. Click Define Groups
  6. Enter the codes for the two groups (e.g., 1 for male, 2 for female)
  7. Click Continue, then OK
Example 1: Gender Differences in TV Watching
  • Dependent variable: tvhours (HOURS PER DAY WATCHING TV)
  • Grouping variable: sex (RESPONDENTS SEX)
  • Group values: 1 = male, 2 = female
Example 2: Internet Users vs. Non-users
  • Dependent variable: tvhours (HOURS PER DAY WATCHING TV)
  • Grouping variable: usenet (USE INTERNET)
  • Group values: 0 = non-users, 1 = users

Interpreting Output:

  • Check Levene's Test for Equality of Variances (Sig. value)
  • If Sig. > 0.05: use "Equal variances assumed" row
  • If Sig. ≤ 0.05: use "Equal variances not assumed" row
  • Check t-test significance (Sig. 2-tailed)
  • If Sig. ≤ 0.05: significant difference exists between groups
  • If Sig. > 0.05: no significant difference between groups

One-Way ANOVA

Test for significant differences among three or more independent groups on a continuous outcome variable.

Primary Dataset: gssnet.sav

SPSS Steps:

  1. Open dataset: gssnet.sav
  2. Click Analyze → Compare Means → One-Way ANOVA
  3. Select dependent variable (continuous outcome measure)
  4. Select factor variable (categorical with 3+ groups)
  5. Click Options
  6. Check "Descriptive" statistics
  7. Check "Homogeneity of variance test"
  8. Check "Means plot" if desired
  9. Click Continue
  10. Click Post Hoc
  11. Check "Tukey" test
  12. Click Continue, then OK
Example 1: Working Hours and Age Categories
  • Dependent Variable: Number of hours worked last week (nethrs)
  • Factor Variable: age category (agecat)
Example 2: Working Hours and Marital Status
  • Dependent Variable: Number of hours worked last week (nethrs)
  • Factor Variable: marital status (marital)

Interpreting Output:

  • Check ANOVA table for Sig. value
  • If Sig. ≤ 0.05: significant differences exist among groups
  • If significant, examine Tukey post hoc tests to identify which specific groups differ
  • Significant differences in Tukey output are marked with an asterisk (*)
  • Examine "Mean Difference" column to understand direction and magnitude of differences

Chi-Square and Crosstabs

Test for associations between categorical variables using crosstabulation and chi-square analysis.

Primary Dataset: gssnet.sav

SPSS Steps:

  1. Open dataset: gssnet.sav
  2. Click Analyze → Descriptive Statistics → Crosstabs
  3. Select row variable (typically dependent)
  4. Select column variable (typically independent)
  5. Click Statistics
  6. Check "Chi-square" for the basic test
  7. For nominal variables: check "Phi and Cramer's V" and/or "Lambda"
  8. For ordinal variables: check "Gamma"
  9. Click Continue
  10. Click Cells
  11. Check "Observed" counts
  12. Check "Column percentages" (most common)
  13. Click Continue, then OK
Example 1: Marital Status and Age Categories
  • Row variable (dependent): marital status (marital)
  • Column variable (independent): age category (agecat)
Example 2: Happiness of Marriage and Age Categories
  • Row variable (dependent): Happiness of Marriage (hapmar)
  • Column variable (independent): age category (agecat)

Interpreting Output:

  • Chi-Square Tests: Check Pearson Chi-Square Sig. value
  • If Sig. ≤ 0.05: significant association exists between variables
  • Effect Size Guidelines:
  • Phi/Cramer's V:
    • 0.00-0.10: Weak association
    • 0.11-0.30: Moderate association
    • > 0.30: Strong association
  • Gamma:
    • < 0.30: Weak association
    • 0.31-0.60: Moderate association
    • > 0.60: Strong association

Correlation

Measure the strength and direction of relationships between continuous variables.

Primary Dataset: gssnet.sav

SPSS Steps:

  1. Open dataset: gssnet.sav
  2. Click Analyze → Correlate → Bivariate
  3. Select variables to correlate (continuous variables)
  4. Ensure "Pearson" is checked for continuous variables
  5. Check "Two-tailed" test of significance (unless you have a directional hypothesis)
  6. Check "Flag significant correlations"
  7. Click OK
Example: Education, Work Hours & Income Correlations

Variables to correlate:

  • Highest year of school completed (educ)
  • Number of hours work last week (hrs1)
  • Respondent's income for last year (rincome)

Interpreting Pearson's Correlation Coefficient (r):

  • Strength guidelines:
    • 0.00-0.10: Negligible correlation
    • 0.11-0.30: Weak correlation
    • 0.31-0.50: Moderate correlation
    • 0.51-0.70: Strong correlation
    • 0.71-1.00: Very strong correlation
  • Direction:
    • Positive r: Variables increase together
    • Negative r: As one increases, the other decreases
  • Statistical significance: Check Sig. (2-tailed) value
    • If Sig. ≤ 0.05: Correlation is statistically significant
    • If Sig. > 0.05: Correlation is not statistically significant

3. Education & Empathy Relationship (30 marks)

Test the relationship between education and Empathy. Is the relationship significant? State the pattern of the relationship.

Required Dataset: attitudes towards people with ID.sav

Steps for Question 1 (Gender & Education):

  1. Open SPSS and load attitudes towards people with ID.sav
  2. Click Analyze → Compare Means → Independent-Samples T Test
  3. Select education (Q12) as the Test Variable
  4. Select gender as the Grouping Variable
  5. Click Define Groups
  6. Enter group codes (e.g., 1 = male, 2 = female)
  7. Click Continue, then OK
  8. In your report, include:
  9. Group statistics (mean education for males and females)
  10. T-test results (t-value, df, significance)
  11. Whether you used equal variances assumed or not (based on Levene's test)
  12. A clear statement of whether the hypothesis is supported
  13. Interpretation of the practical significance of the finding

Steps for Question 2 (Gender & Empathy):

  1. First, recode the reversed empathy items:
  2. Click Transform → Recode into Different Variables
  3. Select empathy1, empathy6, empathy8, empathy9 (reversed items)
  4. Click Old and New Values
  5. Recode values: (0→4, 1→3, 2→2, 3→1, 4→0)
  6. Provide output names (e.g., emp1r, emp6r, emp8r, emp9r)
  7. Click Continue, then OK
  8. Next, compute the total empathy score:
  9. Click Transform → Compute Variable
  10. Target Variable: totalempathy
  11. Numeric Expression: MEAN(empathy2, empathy3, empathy4, empathy5, empathy7, empathy10, emp1r, emp6r, emp8r, emp9r) * 10
  12. Click OK
  13. Run the t-test:
  14. Click Analyze → Compare Means → Independent-Samples T Test
  15. Select totalempathy as the Test Variable
  16. Select gender as the Grouping Variable
  17. Click Define Groups
  18. Enter group codes (e.g., 1 = male, 2 = female)
  19. Click Continue, then OK
  20. In your report, include:
  21. Brief explanation of the recoding and scale computation
  22. Group statistics (mean empathy for males and females)
  23. T-test results (t-value, df, significance)
  24. Whether you used equal variances assumed or not (based on Levene's test)
  25. A clear statement of whether the hypothesis is supported
  26. Interpretation of the differences in empathy by gender

Steps for Question 3 (Education & Empathy):

  1. Method A - Using One-Way ANOVA:
  2. Click Analyze → Compare Means → One-Way ANOVA
  3. Select totalempathy as the Dependent Variable
  4. Select education (Q12) as the Factor
  5. Click Options
  6. Check "Descriptive statistics", "Homogeneity of variance test", and "Means plot"
  7. Click Continue
  8. Click Post Hoc
  9. Select "Tukey" test
  10. Click Continue, then OK
  11. Method B - Using Correlation:
  12. Click Analyze → Correlate → Bivariate
  13. Select totalempathy and education (Q12)
  14. Ensure "Pearson" is checked
  15. Click OK
  16. In your report, include:
  17. Statistics showing the relationship (means by education group or correlation coefficient)
  18. Significance of the relationship
  19. A clear description of the pattern (e.g., "higher education is associated with higher empathy")
  20. Discussion of the practical implications of this relationship
